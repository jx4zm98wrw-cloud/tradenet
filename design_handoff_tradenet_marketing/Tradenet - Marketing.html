Introduction to Tradenet

Tradenet is a search-and-watch tool for the Vietnamese trademark gazette. This guide walks through what the product does, how the four main surfaces fit together, and how to set up your first watchlist in under five minutes.

On this page
  1. What Tradenet does
  2. How the four surfaces fit together
  3. Your first search
  4. What to do next

01 What Tradenet does

Vietnam IP — formally Cục Sở hữu trí tuệ, and previously branded NOIP — publishes a weekly gazette in two sections: Section A for newly-published applications (opening their 5-month opposition window), and Section B for newly-granted registrations. Each issue runs 80–4,000 pages of dense PDF.

Tradenet ingests every issue the day it publishes, extracts the marks, and gives you four surfaces:

  • Today — a digest of what landed since you last checked, anchored on your watchlists
  • Search — text, phonetic, image, and Vienna-code search across the entire corpus
  • Detail — every fact about one mark, including its procedural timeline and opposition window
  • Compare — side-by-side conflict review for 2–3 marks
Tip The fastest way to understand the product is to set up a watchlist for one of your client's brands and let it run for a week. The Monday-morning digest is the moment Tradenet earns its keep.

02 How the four surfaces fit together

The product is built around one loop: watch → find → analyze → act. A watchlist saves a search. Each new gazette issue re-runs the search and surfaces matches on Today. You click into Detail to investigate, optionally pull two or three marks into Compare for a scorecard, then act — usually by filing opposition before the 5-month window closes.

03 Your first search

The fastest way to see Tradenet in action: search for a brand you know, then save the search as a watchlist.

  1. Click Search in the top nav (or press ⌘K)
  2. Switch to the Phonetic / fuzzy mode tab
  3. Type neur* and pick Class 5 in the rail
  4. Drag the similarity threshold up to ~75%
  5. Click Save as watchlist in the active-filters bar

That's it. Every new gazette issue, Tradenet will run that exact query and surface matches on your Today screen.

Your first watchlist

Watchlists are saved queries that re-run automatically against every new gazette issue. Setting one up is the single highest-value action in Tradenet.

01 Pick a brand to watch

Start with a brand you know well — usually one of your client's registered marks. The clearer you are about what conflicts look like, the easier it is to dial in the watchlist's sensitivity.

02 Decide your match criteria

A watchlist combines four kinds of criteria: name (text or phonetic), image (upload a specimen), classes (Nice classification), and applicant filters. Most clearance watchlists use all four.

Recommendation Start broad and tighten. A watchlist that returns 12 false positives the first week is better than one that misses the real conflict on week three.

03 Tune the similarity threshold

The threshold slider controls how aggressive the matcher is. For pharmaceutical brands (Class 5) we recommend starting at 0.75; for retail (Class 35) you can go as low as 0.60 without drowning in noise.

04 Set up alerts

Email digests are on by default. On Firm and Enterprise plans, connect Slack or Microsoft Teams so the whole matter team sees findings in the same place you're already working.

Image similarity search

Image search is the fastest way to find visual lookalikes — including marks that read differently but look the same. It works on uploaded PNGs, JPGs, or PDFs.

01 How it works

When you upload a specimen, three pipelines run in parallel: a perceptual hash for visual similarity, OCR to extract the wordmark text, and a Vienna code classifier for figurative elements. Results combine all three.

ChannelWhat it catchesWeight (default)
pHashVisually identical / near-identical marks40%
OCRMarks that read the same word35%
ViennaSame figurative category (e.g. "stylized V")25%

02 What to upload

  • The cleanest specimen you have — ideally the one you'd submit to Vietnam IP
  • PNG with transparent background works best for visual matching
  • For figurative-only marks (no text), the Vienna classifier carries more weight
  • For wordmarks, OCR-cleanness matters most — avoid stylized fonts when possible
Pro tip You can upload multiple specimens at once and Tradenet will treat them as a single query. Useful for marks with multiple registered variants (color, B&W, with/without tagline).

03 Tuning weights

On the Firm plan and above, you can override the default weights per matter. Pharmaceutical clearance often weights phonetic and visual evenly. Logo conflicts for sports brands weight visual + Vienna heavily, with OCR near zero.

Article 112 — Opposition rules in Vietnam

A practical guide to opposition windows under Vietnamese IP law. Not legal advice — always confirm with qualified counsel, especially for Madrid-protocol designations and edge cases.

01 The 5-month rule

Under Article 112 of the IP Law (as amended 2019, 2022), any third party may file opposition to a published application within five months from the publication date. The clock starts on the date the application appears in the gazette, not the application date.

Important The 5-month window is calendar months, not 150 days. February publications get a slightly shorter window than July ones.

02 What you can oppose on

  • Identity or confusing similarity with an earlier registered or applied-for mark
  • Bad faith (especially common with well-known foreign marks)
  • Descriptive or generic content
  • Conflict with a geographical indication
  • Misleading character (origin, quality, nature of goods)

03 Filing logistics

Oppositions are filed with Vietnam IP, usually through an authorized IP agent. Filing fees are modest (VND 550,000–2,200,000 depending on classes), but the substantive workload — gathering prior-rights evidence, drafting the opposition brief — is where the cost lives.

After the window closes If you miss the 5-month opposition window, your only recourse is invalidation proceedings — slower (typically 18–36 months), more expensive, and with a higher evidentiary bar. The opposition window is genuinely your best shot.

Reference · Vienna 5.1

Vienna classification — figurative codes

The Vienna Classification is the international standard for classifying the figurative elements of trademarks. Tradenet supports search by Vienna code and auto-classifies uploaded images against the current 5.1 edition.

On this page
  1. What Vienna codes are
  2. Reading the code format
  3. Searching by Vienna code
  4. Category reference (1–29)

01 What Vienna codes are

Vienna codes classify the figurative content of a mark — what you see in it, not what it spells. A logo containing a stylized "V" and a circle gets coded against two categories simultaneously. The same mark might carry 5–15 codes in a thorough classification.

The classification is maintained by WIPO and revised every few years. Tradenet uses Vienna 5.1 (2023 revision). Older marks classified under 4.x or 3.x are auto-mapped where the categories are equivalent; flagged for review where they aren't.

02 Reading the code format

Codes are three-level and dotted: CC.DD.SS — Category, Division, Section. For example, 26.04.04 means category 26 (geometric figures), division 04 (quadrilaterals), section 04 (rectangles).

Auxiliary codes A leading A on the last digit (e.g. 26.04.04 A.1) is an auxiliary code — finer-grained sub-classification that's optional but improves recall in image search.

03 Searching by Vienna code

On the Search page, switch to the Vienna code mode tab. You can search by a single full code, a partial code (e.g. 26.04 matches everything under category 26 / division 04), or a combination with boolean operators. 

DSL# Find all marks combining a stylized V with a circle
27.05.21 AND 26.01.03

# All animals (category 3) except common breeds
3.* NOT 3.04.01

# Geometric containers (typeface-agnostic)
24.17.* OR 26.04.*

Reference · Nice 12-2026

Nice classification — goods & services

The Nice Classification is a 45-class system describing the goods and services a trademark covers. A mark registered for "Class 5" (pharmaceuticals) gives you no protection in "Class 30" (foodstuffs) — even for the same wordmark. Getting the class right is the single most common conflict pivot.

On this page
  1. The 45-class split
  2. Commonly confused classes
  3. Searching across classes
  4. Goods reference (1–34)
  5. Services reference (35–45)

01 The 45-class split

Classes 1–34 cover goods (tangible products). Classes 35–45 cover services. The split matters because conflict analysis usually starts with: are these marks in the same or related classes? A pharmaceutical (Class 5) and a restaurant (Class 43) sharing the same name rarely conflict; two food brands in Class 30 almost always do.

02 Commonly confused classes

Three pairs that trip up filers and create avoidable opposition windows:

  • Class 35 (Retail services) vs. the class of the goods sold. A clothing brand selling its own line files in Class 25 (the clothes). A department store selling many brands' clothes files in Class 35.
  • Class 9 (Software) vs. Class 42 (SaaS / cloud). Downloaded apps go in Class 9. Hosted, browser-delivered services go in Class 42. Many filings need both.
  • Class 5 (Pharmaceuticals) vs. Class 44 (Medical services). A drug is a Class 5 good. A clinic that prescribes it is a Class 44 service. Often filed together for healthcare brands.
Cross-class conflict Vietnamese examiners sometimes find conflict across "related classes" even without direct overlap — e.g. Class 32 (beverages) and Class 33 (alcoholic beverages). Tradenet surfaces these via the semantic-similarity channel in the conflict scorecard.

03 Searching across classes

In the search rail, the Nice classes group has an ANY / ALL toggle. Default is ANY — match marks covering any selected class. Switch to ALL when you need marks that cover the full set (e.g. food brands that cover Classes 29, 30, and 32 together).

Vietnam IP glossary & INID codes

The vocabulary of the Vietnamese trademark system — Vietnamese terminology, Vietnam IP's specific conventions, and the WIPO INID numerical field codes that appear in every gazette entry. Bookmark this page.

02 Gazette issue conventions

Vietnam IP publishes a weekly bulletin in two parallel streams. Filenames follow a strict convention: {SECTION}_{ISSUE}_{YEAR}.pdf.

CodeMeaningExample
ASection A · Published applications (opens opposition windows)A_T2_2026.pdf
BSection B · Granted registrations (post-opposition)B_T4_2026.pdf
T1First weekly issue of the month—
T2Second weekly issue of the month—
T3Third weekly issue of the month—
T4Fourth weekly issue of the month—
Application numbers Vietnamese app numbers follow 4-YYYY-NNNNN. The leading "4" is the IP-right type (4 = trademark). Certificate numbers (granted marks) follow 4-NNNNNNN with no year.

03 WIPO INID codes

INID = Internationally agreed Numbers for the Identification of bibliographic Data. Every field in a gazette entry is tagged with a numerical code so the entry can be parsed without knowing the publication's language. Tradenet extracts these directly.

04 International instruments

Vietnam is a member of several international IP treaties that affect how marks are filed and protected.

InstrumentWhat it doesVietnam joined
